Abstract

Accurate and radiation-efficient imaging is essential for neurosurgical interventions involving the spine. This study presents XR2CT, a deep learning framework that reconstructs high-resolution three-dimensional (3D) computed tomography (CT) images from limited two-dimensional (2D) X-ray inputs. A dataset of 1,159 normal and post-operative spine CT scans was used to generate orthogonal synthetic X-ray pairs at 256×256 resolution. XR2CT introduces four key advancements over existing 2D-to-3D reconstruction approaches: (1) increasing output resolution to 256×256, (2) excluding non-informative padded regions using spatial metadata, (3) applying style transfer to harmonize clinical X-rays with standardized synthetic data, and (4) leveraging EfficientNetV2 for computationally efficient and accurate feature extraction. The model is trained with L1 and perceptual losses to preserve both global structures and fine anatomical details. Reconstruction fidelity is evaluated using peak signal-to-noise ratio, structural similarity index, and learned perceptual image patch similarity metrics. Compared to ResNet-101–based models, XR2CT achieves up to 2.8-fold faster training while maintaining superior reconstruction quality, accurately capturing spinal anatomy and hardware positioning. Style transfer mitigates domain discrepancies, enhancing robustness across diverse clinical X-rays. By recovering essential spinal structures from two X-ray views, XR2CT offers a high-resolution reconstruction pipeline that reduces radiation exposure without compromising diagnostic utility. These findings support future advancements in super-resolution reconstruction for refined neurosurgical applications.
